CHAPTER 2: EARTH'S ATMOSPHERE AND CLIMATE

The Composition and Structure of the Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere is a complex mixture of gases that envelop the planet, playing a critical role in supporting life and regulating temperature. It consists primarily of nitrogen (78%) and oxygen (21%), with trace amounts of argon, carbon dioxide, and other gases. The atmosphere is structured into layers based on temperature gradients: the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, thermosphere, and exosphere. Each layer has distinct properties and functions, such as weather phenomena occurring in the troposphere and the ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbing harmful ultraviolet radiation.

The Greenhouse Effect and Climate

The greenhouse effect is a natural process whereby certain gases in the atmosphere trap heat, keeping Earth warm enough to sustain life. Greenhouse gases, including carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapor, absorb and re-radiate infrared radiation, preventing it from escaping into space. Human activities, such as burning fossil fuels and deforestation, have increased the concentration of these gases, leading to enhanced greenhouse effect and global warming. This has significant implications for global climate patterns, sea levels, and ecosystems.

Climate Systems and Patterns

Earth's climate is influenced by various factors, including solar radiation, ocean currents, atmospheric circulation, and geographical features. The planet's climate system is characterized by complex interactions between these components, resulting in diverse climate zones ranging from tropical to polar regions.
