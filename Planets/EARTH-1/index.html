CHAPTER 1: THE FORMATION OF EARTH

The Formation of Earth

Earth formed approximately 4.54 billion years ago from the solar nebula, a giant cloud of gas and dust left over from the formation of the Sun. Through a process called accretion, small particles collided and stuck together, forming planetesimals, which eventually combined to create protoplanets. Earth's early history was marked by frequent collisions and high temperatures, leading to the differentiation of its layers.

The Layers of Earth

Earth is composed of several layers, each with distinct characteristics. The innermost layer is the core, divided into a solid inner core and a liquid outer core, primarily composed of iron and nickel. Surrounding the core is the mantle, a thick layer of silicate minerals that behaves like a viscous fluid over geological timescales. The outermost layer is the crust, a thin, solid shell divided into the oceanic and continental crust. The lithosphere, comprising the crust and upper mantle, is broken into tectonic plates that move over the asthenosphere, a more ductile region of the mantle.

Plate Tectonics

The theory of plate tectonics explains the movement of Earth's lithospheric plates. Driven by convection currents in the mantle, these plates interact at boundaries, leading to seismic activity, mountain building, and the formation of ocean basins. Plate interactions occur in three primary forms: divergent boundaries (where plates move apart), convergent boundaries (where plates collide), and transform boundaries (where plates slide past each other). This dynamic system is crucial for understanding earthquakes, volcanic activity, and the distribution of continents and oceans.
